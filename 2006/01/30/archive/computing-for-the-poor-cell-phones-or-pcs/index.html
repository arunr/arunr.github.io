Computing for the poor - Cell phones or PC's ?

2006-01-30

Read two interesting posts this morning -

  • Scoble’s post on bringing computing to the poor
  • Om Malik’s post (The Daily Cache) which has a link to Grameen Phone’s subscriber numbers
    The Grameen Phone article was especially interesting - discounts & free talk time in order to get more subscribers, this is the way I see growth happening in the cell phone space - lower cost, better service.

Marketeers & technologists in America get excited when they see the cell phone growth & penetration numbers in developing nations, and assume that cell phones will be the best platform to deliver computing power in these countries.

I think the assumption is wrong. To clarify with examples:

1. I work in a Government office as a junior officer. I have a wife and 2 kids. I earn about $200 per month. I want my kids to do well at school & want to get a promotion at work. I have about $200 saved up to spend during the holidays. I would rather buy a computer than a cell phone, though a cell phone will enhance my status among neighbours.

2. I’m a farmer in a small village and have 3 rice fields. I have a wife and 2 kids. I earn about $200 per month. My kids go to school, but they dont learn computers there. I heard about another farmer who uses his phone to find out the price of rice in the city. I have about $200 saved up to spend during the holidays. I would rather buy a cell phone than a computer.

Traditionally, a computer does something distinct from a cell phone. In developing nations, there is still a lot of space for growth by keeping price low and focusing on the basic functionality of the device - great voice and large network at low price in cell phones and basic functionality and reliable connectivity at low price in PCs.

In essence, neither platform is intrinsically better. Computing for the poor? Yea! How should we deliver it? Any & every way we can…
