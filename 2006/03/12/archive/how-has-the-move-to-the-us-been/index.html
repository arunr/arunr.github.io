How has the move to the US been?

2006-03-12

I wrote a long mail to my parents talking about how the move to the US has been, now that we’ve been here for 6 months. Here goes:Dearest Mamma & Pappa,

It has been a little over 6 months since we’ve moved to the US, and I’ve been meaning to write you a long mail letting you know how the move is going for us.

It has been a time of change - a change of place, a change of cultures, a change of work habits and a change of lifestyle.

The changes have been exciting - there are new things we learn daily, there are new sights, smells and sounds and many new things to do.

The changes have also been sobering - we’re reminded daily of the things we knew before we left that we going to give up, and we discover daily many more things we never realized we were going to lose. Each of these come with their own pang of longing to be back.

So, what is it we’ve enjoyed?

We’ve enjoyed the place - The Northwest is beautiful, and we’ve enjoyed simply being here and having trees around us, beautiful lakes nearby and mountains in the not-so-far distance. We’ve gone on many hikes and each time we talk about how quickly we can get to a terrific hike that has both stunning views and that offers great exercise.

We’ve enjoyed ourselves at work - both Kanchuki & I are doing well at work. We both are enjoying the products & problems we’re working on and have super managers who are keenly interested in our growth. That Microsoft has 35,000 people here and has been around for 30 years means we have more to learn from, more opportunities to choose from and more structure to help us do our best. Most importantly - we usually come in to work at 9 and leave at 6 - a huge change from the hours we were keeping in India.

I guess we’ve also enjoyed the power of the dollar and the power of choice - we have a car we like, a big house with a yard at the back and we’ve traveled to Peru for 20 days, things we wouldn’t been able to afford in India. We’ve saved more in 6 months here than we would have in India – that is a cold fact.

However, though we’ve enjoyed this choice, we’ve also been maddened by it - the range of things to choose from drives us nuts and the incessant consumption we see around us makes us dizzy. We try to avoid spending time at malls and when we do we fear that we’re becoming addicted to shopping! We see the Grande Lattes, double portions at restaurants and gas guzzling cars and are saddened by the waste of it all.

In terms of lifestyle and culture, the change has been surprising. We lead a more regular, steady and sober life than we did in India! On weekdays, we get up early, we take our lunch with us to work, we get back soon and we eat dinner at home. We meet friends over the weekend, we go out on Saturdays and spend Sundays cleaning the home and resting. Compare that with India - friends dropping in at any time, going to eat out on the spur of the moment to a new place, sleeping at 3:00 am because we were talking to friends and waking up and rushing to work at 11:00 am!

People and things here are conservative, steady and sure, while in India they are exciting, changing and random.

Now, what is it that we’ve missed?

We’ve missed family - Mamma, Pappa, Ma, Deuta and Aijoni - and daily realize how much we miss you, even more than we miss our friends. In India we generally ignored you and spent all our time with friends (!), simply because of the comfort of your being nearby and of us being able to get up and come to visit you immediately if the need ever arose (I felt even more so when Akka passed away).

We’ve of course missed friends too – Lolly, AKB, Rajeev, Smriti, DKC, Trishna, Sridala, Asim, Kapil, Chow, Vandana – the list is long. We miss meeting with our friends, talking, singing, going out and having fun every day of the week.

We miss playing Basketball in the Hyderabad heat, drinking lassi with malai & kulfi, eating Andhra Thalis and the smell of Hyderabadi Biryani!

We miss our amma who cleaned the house, the infuriating auto-wallas and the innumerable people we used to have to interact with daily for the smallest of things.

We miss the “not knowing in the morning what the evening will hold” that used to happen in India.

We miss the excitement, the energy and the aspirations of growth that is evident in India.

There are two things I’ve learnt from the move -

While in India I considered myself a global citizen, in the US I’m more aware that I’m Indian. I understand what my country means to me, of her place in the world and my Indian-ness.

Also, while moving, I thought of the move as being an easy one to which we would easily acclimatize, given our upbringing, exposure and western outlook. I’ve realized however that we are immigrants – the longing we feel is longing of the dispossessed, trying to fit in with the new and holding on to memories of the old.

Overall, the move has been a good one – not better or worse in the balance, but a move that we’ve learnt a lot from and a change that has been positive in us now having seen & traveled more of the world.

We’ll talk more about these things when you come here – we’re both looking forward to your coming here and spending time with us for at least 4 weeks,

Love,
arun
