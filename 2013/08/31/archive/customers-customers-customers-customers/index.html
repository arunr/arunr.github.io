Customers, customers, customers, customers

2013-08-31

Here’s my first post in the Daily Product Management series. Here’s a cartoon to get your appetite going…


Get real customers by appa

Whether in a start-up, or in a big product company, there is one thing that every product manager should cultivate: a deep connection with one real, paying customer.

Though this seems simple and obvious, it is very hard. Here is a list of excuses:




“I know what the customer wants because….”: No, you don’t. Find a real, paying customer. “Once we build it the customers will come….””: No, they wont. Find a real, paying customer.
“Getting the product out is more important than finding a customer…”: No, it isn’t. Find a real, paying customer. “I once worked in the same industry, so I understand…”: No, you don’t. Find a real, paying customer.
“I have done a survey and secondary research, and it says…”: *I don’t care! Find a real, paying customer.

Also, here are a few tips in working with real customers that I have found useful:

>

>
>
> Don’t try and find many real, paying customers: Finding one (or three) is sufficient. This is not about quantitative research. It is about understanding real people.
> Cultivate the relationship over time: You should connect with the same customer over the course of a year. You should meet them every month. You should should call them every week.
> Don’t be in a hurry: Be patient. You are going to gain a deep knowledge of your customers lives, an empathy towards her challenges and insights into what solutions will work for her. Don’t rush it.
> “I have a customer, but he isn’t right, he just doesn’t understand…”: He is right. How do you pick the right customer? If they are real, and paying - they are right. Nothing else counts.
> * Put it on your calendar: If it’s not on your calendar, you won’t do it. So, schedule your weekly time with your customer.
>
>



Here’s my last tip: learn to listen. Set up your customer meeting at a time when you are most relaxed and open to listening (perhaps after some meditation or a nice breakfast). When you meet with them, be calm and present, listen to their concerns, and chat casually, with no agenda, no problem solving and no real care for the outcomes. Just a nice, good conversation.

If nothing else, practising this with customers will surely help you in any relationship you are in too!
