Book review: Nudge

2011-03-04

I re-read the book Nudge after hearing about it by a talk by John Thackara at the UnBox festival last weekend, a quick review. The ideas in the book that I found interesting:

  • People can be nudged into making choices that they might otherwise may not have (for example, placement of ice-cream on the menu determines how many will order ice-cream).
  • The core concept of “libertarian paternalism” – that is, people should be free to choose, but choice architects should nudge them towards better choices.
  • The idea that every designer ends up constraining the freedoms and making decisions for others on their choices – this was disturbing to me, since I’m such a freedom-nut, but I could grasp the truth of this.
  • The concept of humans and econs – humans being blundering and unable to stick to their resolutions, econs being the model of humans in economics (of being rational beings).
  • The concept of the reptilian brain and the mammalian brain (the automatic and reflective systems).
  • The way biases happen – anchoring, availability, representativeness, optimism and overconfidence, gains and losses, status quo bias and framing.
  • Inability to resist temptation, and how peer pressure, bets and mental accounting can help you regain self-control (the concept of a doer and a planner in the same body!)
  • Herd mentality, the spotlight effect and the power of a smile or a frown.

  • When should we give a nudge?

    • Benefits now, costs later situations (drinking and driving)
    • As the degree of difficulty of a task increases – selecting a health plan
    • Encouraging Frequency / Practice
    • Feedback about options we reject
    • Knowing what you like in unfamiliar environments

    • The model for choice architecture:

      *   iNcentives
      • Understand mappings (the picture size example instead of megapixels in cameras)
      • Defaults
      • Give feedback
      • Expect error

      • Structure complex choices

      • The understanding of incentives – who uses, who chooses, who pays, who profits?

    • How collective intelligence and collaborative filtering algorithms are aiding structuring of complex choices.
      Overall, this is a good book that I would recommend for a quick read.
