Product Management

Writing clearly, writing often

2011-02-05

Call me a dinosaur, but writing clearly and writing often are among the basic skills that a program manager* needs to have in her toolkit. And I don’t mean text messages, twitter or even your emails.

“Bt y? Wrtng is over8’d!!”, you say.

New product design and development is ambiguous. Reducing ambiguity and increasing clarity are at the heart of the program managers job. How do you reduce ambiguity & increase clarity, especially when you don’t have a clue and don’t really know much more than the next person?

On days, it can feel like catching soap bubbles before they burst. How do you retain your sanity amidst the madness and transform yourself into a beacon of clarity & calm?

“Write it down, write it down!”, one of my earlier managers used to exhort - and he was absolutely right. Make a start, write it down, write clearly & write often - that’s my mantra!

The miracle of it is that something as simple as writing it down can be transformational.

It goes something like this for me: I make some time, sit down and start writing down all my thoughts. “Ah, dang - that can’t be true, I wrote the opposite thing above!”, I think and scratch something out. A while later it is “Oh, gee, that’s scary - I didn’t realize that was such a big problem!”. A while later I am done, and I’m feeling much better - I just got all my thoughts out (it really is a “brain dump”!), I understand the big issues and I have a starting place from which to talk to people.

Also, I’ve lost count of the number of times that I wake up the next morning and think “Ah, I got it - that thing I thought was a big problem - I know how that can be done!” - writing it down is an aid to serendipity!

The fact is that the very exercise of making an effort to write things down brings more clarity and helps clarify your own point of view. This then allows you to review what you have written with the other. In meetings, people feel engaged and happy when they see that you’ve come in with something written down to go over, and they also are willing to take a bet that you know more than them!

“Ah, thank God, it won’t be one of those talk-talk-talk meetings again!” they think to themselves.

You have a good meeting and you refine what you wrote down based on the feedback you received - by now, you’re feeling great about what you have.

As you refine, review and update, you start to feel even better - the writing seems to be writing itself down! The effort you need to make reduces, and you get into a flow. The work is doing itself, and there is plenty time for golf in the afternoon, beer in the evenings and a happily-ever-after ending (OK, I think I may have overdone the sell job there!).

Here are my tips & tricks for writing, in the context of a product development job:

  1. Know what writing means: By writing, I don’t mean text messages, tweets, blogs or email. I mean a clear, concise document, presentation a spreadsheet model or a even a picture with thinking about what needs to be built to solve a customers problem. It shouldn’t be long, verbose or filled with words that show off your geek quotient! Simple, simple, simple language - please!
  2. Calm & quiet: Writing cannot be done in haste or under stress. Find a block of at least hours, a quiet coffee shop and a time when you are feeling calm.
  3. Write it down, no matter how little: Write down what you know. It doesn’t matter how little you know or how big the problem is. Write your thoughts down.
  4. Basic questions: Think through the basic questions - why, who, what, when, where, how. This will help you refine your writing.
  5. Things you don’t know: Make a list of the things you don’t know or need to find out, and put that in the document. As you find things out, strike them off - it’ll make you feel great!
  6. Write mindfully: When you’re writing, just write. Don’t get distracted and try solving the problem, formatting the text color or paragraph spacing or searching the Internet for pictures to put in. Make one editorial pass, but only when you are done writing.
  7. Don’t over-spend time writing: I know people who take their writing seriously, and will write a product feature definition for weeks! This is crazy, because you can’t solve it yourself. I would strongly urge not spending more than a couple of hours at most at each writing pass. Don’t do more than four or five passes, interspersed with review meetings and feedback.
    I have listed below my personal favorite books on learning the art of writing - they aren’t limited to technical writing, but are just great references whether you want to write a technical document or the next great novel!
  • The Elements of Style, Strunk and White: An absolute classic!
  • Writing down the bones, Natalie Goldberg: Follow this, and you will write better. The heart of all art is practice.
  • Zen in the Art of Writing, Ray Bradbury: Tips from a master of sci-fi.
  • On Writing, Stephen King: You wouldn’t expect it from Stephen King of all authors - but a great, personal look at writing.
  • The Non-Designers Design Book, Robin Williams: A good guide to learning the essentials of typography & clear layout.
    * Note: I have used the term program manager to refer to a specific role at Microsoft. In many other technology companies this role is referred to as product manager.
