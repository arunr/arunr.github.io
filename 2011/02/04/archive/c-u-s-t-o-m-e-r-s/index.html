Product Management

C.U.S.T.O.M.E.R.S

2011-02-04

CUSTOMERS, customers, the heart of all product development. Never forget this! Customers are the people, each individual, unique, quirky and wonderful, that we build our products for. We don’t build products for business or niche segments or target groups - we build products for people!

“OK, Arun, so customers are important”, you say. “But how should I use this in building products?”

This is easy, but also extremely hard.

It is easy, because you don’t need to do much -

  1. You need to cultivate an interest in people.
  2. You need to quietly and deeply observe people and listen to them in their natural settings.
  3. You need to get out of your offices and talk to customers often.
    It is also really hard - because it is easy to ignore customers. It is easy to ignore them by making up creative stories about what customers want, it is easy to ignore customers for the love of the shiny, new technology, it is easy to ignore customers in the excitement of making money, and it is easy to ignore customers in the haste of getting things out of the door.

These things (creativity, technology, business, shipping) are all important, but they are harmful to the one thing that really matters: building products that customers will desire.

So, slow down, get out of your ivory towers and talk to people, observe them in their natural settings, listen to them and then, only then, start thinking about what you will build in the product.

Don’t stop there, though - go out often, and meet customers and observe people throughout the product development cycle, show them what you have and refine your decisions, and by golly, even change the product if what you have built doesn’t work for them. One word of caution though - don’t ask customers “What do you want in the next version of my product?”. They don’t know that, and that’s your job to find out - not by asking, but by observing and listening with unbiased eyes & ears.

Here are my three easy tips for learning more about people:

  • Mall-watching: A favorite pastime of mine. Get out of your office, go to a mall and hang out for a few hours on your own. Get a large drink to sip on, find a place to sit and watch people! Technically, this is called ethnography - I refer to it as fun!
  • Be (more) curious: Be more curious about people - when talking to them, don’t just talk about their work or how many kids they have! That kind of talk is for old people!! Ask them why they wear their pants so high, ask them what deodorant they are using, ask them when they leave for work or how they shave their beards! You will learn a lot about people this way, and will also have more fun in your conversations.

  • Travel often, and travel widely: Traveling to new places on vacation, when you are calm and observant, is the best way to learn about different people. However, one word of caution - don’t do the same thing every time you travel. It is easy to say “Oh, I went to Goa last year, this year I am going to Maldives, that’s different, right?”. No it isn’t, especially if you are staying in a resort! Travel widely - go to cities, to villages, to slums and to, OK, resorts too. Travel in a group, travel alone, travel with little money in your pocket! Stay with a family, stay in a seedy motel, camp out in the open. These experiences, and the variety of people you meet will serve you well in your work.
    Technically, all of this is called ethnography. I call it pure, unadulterated fun! To close, I leave you with some homework!
