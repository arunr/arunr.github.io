Product Management

Having ideas: A conversation

2011-02-17

Recently, I asked a friend what 5 things she would do if she were the designer of the next version of the iPhone, her favorite gadget. “Well,” she started off, “there is the phone itself, photos, music, all the apps and the GPS, let me think of improvements in each area!”.

“Wait a second,” I said, “What you’re doing - that’s great, but it’s what we program managers call feature thinking! There are at least 3 other ways to think about the same problem and generate ideas - there’s customer thinking, ecosystem thinking and then the scenario approach.”

“Huh, how do those work - are they any better?” she asked.

“Well, they all are different models, and provide some structure for thinking and idea generation,” I replied, “But I have found that the scenario approach is great for coming up with new and innovative ideas, customer thinking is good for exploring new segments, ecosystem thinking is good for getting the big picture and feature thinking allows you to think through evolutionary improvements to the existing features.”

“They all are useful in their own way,” I continued, “and in my opinion, you need all of them. However, I think most engineers end up being too feature-thinking oriented and mostly make incremental improvements. They don’t really exercise the customer thinking or ecosystem thinking parts of the brain and rarely use the scenario approach! These alternatives allow creation of new or non-linear growth in value for the business, and feature-only thinking can lead to missing out on new opportunities. That’s where we program managers come in”, I added, justifying the reasons for our existence!

“OK, I didn’t understand, give me an example”, she said.

“Let us take customer thinking. Instead of starting with the features areas like phone, photos in the iPhone, you could have started with different types of customers who use the iPhone. So you could’ve said - see, the iPhone is used by young people who want to look cool, working professionals who have the money, by students in school or college, by the elderly.”

“Hmm, I see, so what’s the difference?”

“Well, when you dive in to each, you may then have ideas that you may not naturally have picked out in your features-oriented thinking. So, you may say that for young people who want to look cool, we should have different colors of phones and engravings on the back of the phone, which you may not have identified as a feature. You may come up with an idea around having larger fonts for the elderly - again, not something that may have come up as a priority in the feature oriented thinking.”

“Wow, I see that,” she said, getting excited, “So, I may come up with a completely different set of ideas!”.

“Yeah,” I replied, “and ecosystem thinking is also like that, except that you look at the big picture then, rather than the customers.”

“Big picture, I hate that phrase, what does it mean?”

“Well, see - instead of thinking of the features or customers, you can think of the industry as a whole, and come up with categories like this: there are the developers who build apps, there are the phone networks, there is direct competition from other phone device manufacturers and operating systems, there is indirect competition from platforms such as Facebook or solutions like Skype, there are also interesting technology developments to think of - like the growth of Groupon, the whole places focus with Foursquare and so on. Then there is competition for the customers dollar, in terms of purchasing similar consumer electronic devises, so even the PSP or XBox are competitors!”

“Wow, that’s a mouthful, but I get the idea, you can now come up with ideas in each of those?”

“Yeah, so you can then think through what are the next set of things to do for developers or phone networks, or how to compete with XBox or PSP. That will lead to an entirely different set of ideas than either the feature or customer thinking!”.

“Interesting, really interesting - I didn’t realize we could think of the same problem from so many different sides. What is the scenario approach you spoke of, and why didn’t you call it scenario thinking?”

“Well, you can call it whatever you want,” I said, “the reason I called it approach was that it builds on customer thinking or ecosystem thinking, and so it is more broad and deep than feature, customer or ecosystem thinking.”

“I don’t understand what’s different - give me an example.”

“Well, firstly, it is more real - I don’t like the feature, or customer or ecosystem kind of thinking in idea generation, because mostly you are sitting in your office or in a meeting room, in your ivory tower, brainstorming ideas and making up stuff on behalf of the customer or the partner. You then go and validate whether what you found is true or not, and there is a strong bias towards being right, in validating the ideas you came up with yourself.”

“Give me an example I said, and stop talking so much!”

“OK, calm down. See, let us say you go and observe some set of people who are using their iPhones. Let us say you pick people who are going on a holiday, and who take photos with their iPhone when they are on vacation. You observe them, and see that most of them take a photo, and then navigate to the photo gallery. They then delete it immediately if they don’t like the photo, or they upload to Facebook if they like it. You may observe that tagging the photos, adding a title and setting the location are all hard.”

“Then?”

“Well, then nothing - you just note down this common scenario, and other scenarios like this, and then come up with a set of ideas or features within the scenario. So, in this case you may come up with the idea that let us make it easy to review and delete photos immediately after they are taken, or let us make it easy to add the location information from the GPS when a photo is taken.”

“Hmm, interesting, but why is it different? I could have come up with the same ideas when doing my feature thinking and looking only at the photos feature.”

“Two reasons I find it different.

Firstly, it gives me great comfort in knowing that I’m not making up stories, but rather finding out what real users or real partners in the ecosystem do by observing them and listening to them and writing down the entire flow they go through before coming up with ideas.

Secondly, I find that with feature, or customer or ecosystem thinking I usually come up with ideas that optimize around the first step of the user interaction (like bigger fonts for the elderly!), without really thinking through optimizing the entire set user interaction patterns, which scenario thinking allows me to do (like bigger devices for the elderly, with a real keyboard instead of touch).”

“Very interesting, that is really cool, so you use scenario thinking for generating new and innovative ideas, customer thinking for exploring new segments, ecosystem thinking for the big picture and feature thinking for incremental improvements”

“Ah, no - I lied!,” I said, “Don’t do that - instead, what I’d recommend is this - think through, but not for too long, your features, your target customer segments and your ecosystem to understand what your product does, what customers want and what your partner needs are. Then go out and meet with real customers and partners and observe them in the wild, like going to a zoo! Then always, always use the scenario approach - it is the one that generates the broadest and deepest set of ideas, if you have done the right groundwork before-hand.”

“OK - so what’s my key take-away?”, she asked.

“Key take-away, I hate that phrase!”, I smiled, and said, “Use the scenario approach. May the force be with you.”
