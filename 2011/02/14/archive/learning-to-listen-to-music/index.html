Learning to listen to music

2011-02-14

Dear Nika,

It makes me happy to see how much you like listening to music! Music is happiness, music is a balm, music is magic!

When I was younger, I used to claim tastes and preferences in music that would give me identity - “I listen to sixties rock-and-roll,” I used to say, or “My favorite type of music is swamp rock”. This was a way to look cool, express my sense of self, be part of a group or worse, subtly exclude someone from a group - none of which had anything to do with music.

I am older now, more comfortable with who I am, not having to be someone else for the sake of others.

Now, at last, I finally can just listen to music, without any of my pride getting in the way!

In listening to music with new ears, I have realized something wonderful - that any type of music is a joy. Also, I have learnt a lot from you about how to really listen to music - I am not as stiff as I used to be, and I now listen to music with ears, my heart, my whole body, moving and grooving to the tunes, the way you do! I am also more plebeian in my tastes, I listen to anything -

Bollywood, hip-hop,

Carnatic, pop

Hindustani, rock & roll,

Classical, soul!
I want to leave you today with a story about the wonder and power of music, a story of bravery about the Cellist of Sarajevo, Vedran Smailovic. Sarajevo is a country that was torn by war, and one day a bomb fell on a line of people waiting to get bread. Twenty-two people were killed.

Vedran was a violin player who played for the Sarajevo Orchestra, and he was very angry about the war, and very sad for the people who had died waiting in line for food.

Vedran took his cello to the crater left by the bomb, and stayed there in between the gunfire and played music for 22 days, one day for each of the people who had died in the bomb blast.

The music he played was a wonderful western classical piece called Adagio in G Minor, you can listen to here here.

When I heard this story for the first time, tears came to my eyes - what is it that makes a man go out into the streets, where there is threat to his own life and sit down calmly and play music? I think it is some deep realization that in the end, when the war is over, when the tears have dried and the wounds have healed - there will still be music, timeless and immortal.

To me, what Vedran did was a brave act of protest, a way of telling people that though they may kill each other, they wouldn’t be able to kill the music that played in him, and that he played for the world.

That, Dear Nika, is the wonder and power of music - I haven’t learnt to play any music yet, but if there is one skill I want for you, it is for you to be able to play an instrument and make your own music.

Keep jiving, keep grooving and keep dancing and signing along with an open heart to all that wonderful music!
