Product Management

Making better decisions

2011-02-10

Building new products has a striking similarity with investing in the stock-market: if you have perfect information, then it’s probably worthless. Many format wars (Blu-Ray or HD-DVD? VHS or BetaMax?) costing customers and companies millions of dollars would be unnecessary if there was prior perfect information.

In building products, you will not have perfect information. This is a given.

“Then how can we decide anything?”, you ask. The answer is really simple - “Guess, and then revise!”.

This was very hard for me to understand or digest when I was a journeyman in product development. I wanted to be sure that the decisions I made were right! Oh, what misery that caused - hours of reading, researching, modeling and thinking, procrastinating, wrangling over decisions and flip-flopping!

And all to what end? To get it wrong in the end, most of the time anyway!

“I am not here to play dice or poker, I am here to build products,” I thought, “I am an engineer, a programmer at heart at that: things have to be rational, logical, decisions have to be binary, false or true, zero or one!”.

Being a journeyman, I also had a lot of pride in my decisions - I never revised them once they were done! I would fight to the end, defending and giving reasons for why the decision was the best.

I learnt a few things along the way that have helped me become more calm at making decisions fast - sharing those stories here.

“Make forward progress daily!”, my manager yelled at me one day.

“But how?”, I asked, “How can I know for sure?”.

“The best way to make decisions is to make them! And you can’t be sure!!”, he replied. Seeing my puzzled face, he slowed down and said, “Make a few decisions every day, things that move the product along. You’ll soon find that the more decisions you’re making, the better you get at making decisions.”

“Anyway, you’ll probably get most of them wrong,” he added, injuring my pride, “But you’re here to learn, not to get everything right!”.

The penny dropped then for me - the freedom to make mistakes was liberating, and a burden lifted off my shoulders. I hadn’t realized till then that it was OK to get things wrong!

“Developing your internal compass”, my manager used to call it, and I became fearless in taking more and more decisions. For sure, I made a lot of mistakes, but I also got a few things right along the way.

The amazing thing was that I learnt so much once I was liberated from the responsibility of being right: I learnt when to trust my gut, and when to be wary of it, I learnt what information to listen to and what to discard as useless, I learnt who to talk to and who would confuse me further!

Along the way, I also chanced upon a wonderful article in HBR by Peter Drucker, called “Managing Oneself“. A particular paragraph in this article caught my eye. Drucker writes, “Every time you make a key decision, write down the outcome you expect. Several months later, compare the actual results with your expected results.”

So simple, and it made total sense. How better to improve at decision making than reviewing how you’ve done? I realized I was doing just the opposite by being proud of my decisions and never revisiting them - I was being egg-headed, and would often ignore the actual outcome of my decisions, sometimes even deluding myself by thinking “I was right, that was how the result should have played out - it is the system is flawed!”.

Once I got into the habit of reviewing my decisions based on actual fact, and not attaching stories to my failures, my capacity to see what things contributed to my making good decisions and what things caused me to make poor decisions improved dramatically. For example, and this may sound dumb - but it’s true - I realized that I made quick and bad decisions when I hadn’t had much sleep! It sounds obvious, but only by really learning to observe my decision making process did I get better at making decisions.

All of this of course is work in progress - I still have a long way to go, and a lot to improve in my decision making ability.

“Not always so,” says Shunryu Suzuki when asked to explain the essence of Buddhism, “Let go”, he adds.

If this is applicable to a great religion like Buddhism, it definitely also touches the heart of decision making!

The way I understand it is this: make decisions, review them without ego when new information comes in, change course if the new information demands it, and let go of the stories you are making up to defend past decisions.

This is common sense. However, it is easy for your mind to get clouded with ego, and for you to close your eyes and stop seeing the truth, leading to one poor decision being defended or leading to other poor decisions.

Here is my self made, pop-science list of things I guarantee will improve your decision making (and which will make you happier anyway if they don’t work!)

  • Sleep 8 hours a day.
  • Cut down on coffee, and drink more water.
  • Exercise daily.
  • Get some quiet time daily to do nothing - take a walk, meditate, lounge about.
  • Review your decisions periodically.
  • Learn to listen to your heart.
    I also liked these tips on http://www.zenhabits.net on “The Fine Art of Decision Making“, worth a read!

Now go on, make those decisions that will change the world!
