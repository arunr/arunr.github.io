Product Management

Saturn dreaming of Mercury

2011-02-12

“I enjoy program management,” I tell people, “because it’s all about making tough trade-offs between customer needs, technology & business goals”. Recently, I heard the same idea expressed as design thinking. Tim Brown says this in his bok “Change by Design“ about design thinking:

… a talent for balancing technical, commercial and human considerations
How does one achieve this balance? It is not easy - as a designer, it is hard to give up on any of these considerations, you wan’t the best for your customer, the best technology and a brilliant business of course.

But then, who wants an easy job?

How do you choose between two equally good or equally bad options, given limited information? You could guess - toss a coin, maybe - and this works about 50% of the time! However, I believe that by doing the hard work of making tough calls and keep tab, you can increase this percentage significantly, maybe to get it right sixty or seventy percent of the time.

The reason that many program managers go home and have a drink each evening is because they can view themselves as being in a no-win situation: you either have to give up something dear to you on behalf of the business, or give up something dear to you in terms of the technology or, much worse, give up something on behalf of the customer.

Leadership coaches and negotiation instructors can talk all day long about creating “win-win” outcomes, but designers actually doing the real work of building products will tell you that this is bullpoop. The reality is that product design is a depressing downward slope from planning to shipping, from the creative to the mundane.

In a great book by Lewis Hyde called “Trickster makes this world“, he has this phrase which I remember - in talking about his own way of writing books and about creativity in general, he calls himself:

Saturn dreaming of mercury
I could totally relate to this: imagine someone saturnine, gloomy, moody and depressive dreaming of being mercurial, inventive, swift and sharp.

To me, this really captured the heart of a program managers job - to be Saturn dreaming of Mercury at all times, to talk eagerly about how we could do such-and-such for the customer or do so-and-so for the business during planning, only to then buckle down and not do most of it, to focus on building much less and then shipping, shipping.

The hardest part of the job for me is fighting with your heart for every decision, and then letting go and not caring about the decision that is made.

In the beginning of the post I talked about enjoying program management, and about who wants an easy job. Well, that was bravado. I lied. I am masochistic, perhaps.

How do you find the balance, to care deeply about the roll of the dice, and yet to move on without looking once the dice has fallen?

I know I haven’t yet found that balance.
