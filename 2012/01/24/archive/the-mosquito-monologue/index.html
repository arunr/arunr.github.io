The mosquito monologue

2012-01-24

I was not able to sleep much last night. Mosquitoes buzzed near my ears, and I tossed and turned, hurling abuse and slapping about in the dark. Whenever I was successful, I hoped that this one would be the last one. The mosquitoes nagged my wife and daughter too, and I heard them toss about. My daughter scratched herself where she had been bitten, and pressed herself closer to me, trying to hide her head near my body where the mosquitoes couldn’t get her.

I finally woke up and turned on the lights. With the electric tennis-bat like apparatus that is used to fry mosquitoes out of existence, I wandered about in a dazed state, trying to find the nocturnal terrorists. I didn’t spot a single one, despite examining the corners, tapping the back of the bed with the racket and climbing on to the bed and peering at the ceiling. After fifteen minutes of this somnambulent mission, I crept back into bed and turned off the lights. Within minutes the buzzing began again.

Where had they been hiding? How did they know that I was after them when the lights came on? I was sure that they had evolved - I know that 3 months earlier when I woke up, I used to be able to find and exterminate all foul flighted foes. Now, not single one. My suspicion was from the previous days few lucky kills. The mosquitoes I used to kill 3 months ago used to be big, used to sit on the ceiling or on the mirror (narcissistic blood-suckers!) and didn’t use to buzz as much as they bit. The mosquitoes I had killed yesterday were tiny things, with a big buzz and the one that I had killed had been hiding on the side of the bed, it’s dark body camouflaged against the dark brown of the bed.

I got up again and my wife cursed at me. “Don’t make so much noise, it is impossible to sleep, kill them silently.” My daughter turned and kicked, her leg hitting my back and giving impetus to my resolve to kill the mosquitoes before I slept today.

Lights on. No mosquitoes. Ticking clock. Peaceful snores of my wife. Red bite marks on my daughters cheek. Tired, I am so tired.

 

I creep out of the room to my daughters bed room, throw all her toys off her tiny bed and crawl into it. I pull the coverlet over me completely, just in case there were mosquitoes here. I felt craven. I had deserted my family, that was at this very instance being fed upon by roving rhino-raptors. I fell asleep soon, with a deep guilt in my gut. I was woken up by an incessant buzzing near my ears. “What the?”, I moaned. It couldn’t have been more than ten minutes that I had slept. My eyes stuck together, refusing to open. I felt a proboscis piercing my right ear and I slapped at it. How could this be? The coverlet was new and had no holes. I had completely covered myself with it, with not a single place from which a mosquito could enter.

The buzzing stopped. I felt lost. I lay awake in fear. I wanted to sleep, but I was afraid to doze. Not a sound. I strained my ears. Silence. Then, a faint buzz. In the distance, it wasn’t near. I made sure that the coverlet was tight around my body. I thought of my daughter, and the bite marks on her cheek. I thought of my wife and her disturbed sleep. Silence. I heaved a sigh of relief. Suddenly, a loud buzzing near my ear. I slapped about in anger and in vain. My sudden animated action had opened up holes in the carefully closed coverlet. I felt like weeping. I considered getting my mp3 player and wearing my headphones - I possibly could sleep while being bitten, but I definitely could not stand the buzzing.

I finally dropped off to sleep, tired and desolate. I woke up, the watery sun of the winter morning streaming golden slants of light on my face. I felt a hundred years old. My daughters toys lay strewn on the floor, with the mosquito-killing tennis racket on top of them. I had no recollection of bringing the tennis racket here. I pushed myself out of bed, walked out into the hall and looked at the clock.

7:45 am. No running today. No writing today in the morning. Blasted mosquitoes. My stomach felt weak. This wouldn’t be a good day. I pushed forward into the kitchen to make myself a cup of tea.

I was tired, angry and afraid. I thought of what I would do tonight, perhaps a mosquito coil (though they seemed resistant to that now), or maybe more vigilance and violence early in the evening - shut the windows and kill all the mosquitoes before going to bed.

Tonight will be another night of waiting, watching, wakefulness and possibly weeping.

The war will continue.
